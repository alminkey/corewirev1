# Editorial Narrator Pass Design

**Date:** 2026-04-08  
**Status:** Approved for planning  
**Scope:** `CoreWire Analysis` flagship writer voice and composition only

> **Supersession note:** This document supplements [2026-04-01-corewire-analysis-doctrine-design.md](/f:/2026/CoreWire/CooreWireV1/docs/superpowers/specs/2026-04-01-corewire-analysis-doctrine-design.md), [2026-04-06-flagship-insight-engine-design.md](/f:/2026/CoreWire/CooreWireV1/docs/superpowers/specs/2026-04-06-flagship-insight-engine-design.md), and [2026-04-07-lead-insight-flagship-design.md](/f:/2026/CoreWire/CooreWireV1/docs/superpowers/specs/2026-04-07-lead-insight-flagship-design.md). The doctrine and insight engine stay in place. This slice changes how flagship analysis sounds and flows on the page.

## Goal

Turn flagship `CoreWire Analysis` from a structurally correct analysis into an article that sounds like a finished editorial product.

The target is not:

- more sections
- more actor coverage
- more explicit scaffolding
- more visible system logic

The target is:

- a stronger opening lead
- a more magazine-like narrative voice
- fewer template-shaped actor blocks
- a harder, cleaner closing cadence

## Problem Statement

The current writer now does several important things correctly:

- isolates a lead insight
- distinguishes visible frame from deeper struggle
- adds contradiction, timing, and consequence layers
- blocks weak flagship output more aggressively

But the resulting article still often feels:

- too thesis-first
- too list-shaped in the middle
- too repetitive
- too close to an internal intelligence memo

The article is analytically better than before, but it still does not feel like something a reader would remember for its writing as well as its argument.

## Recommended Approach

Use an `Editorial Narrator Pass` layered on top of the current flagship insight writer.

The writer should keep the same underlying structure:

- lead insight
- visible frame
- contradiction
- timing
- proof stack
- consequence
- hard ending

But the surface delivery must change:

- the opening must read like a lead, not a thesis engine
- the middle must read like an argument, not a list of actor states
- the ending must feel like an editorial close, not a tidy unresolved summary

## Opening Rule

The first two paragraphs must feel like a magazine lead.

That means:

- shorter sentence rhythm
- one unmistakable angle
- no `The crisis is escalating because...`
- no explanatory throat-clearing

The lead insight may still exist as an internal writer artifact, but the visible output should open with a sharper editorial sentence, usually one of these shapes:

- `The real danger is not X. It is Y.`
- `What looks like X is becoming Y.`
- `The war is already changing more than the battlefield.`
- `The confrontation is exposing a problem that the public language is still hiding.`

The second paragraph can then widen into context.

## Middle-Section Rule

The middle of the article should no longer default to:

- `X wants...`
- `X is under pressure from...`
- `X next move is likely...`

That shape exposes the machine.

Instead, the middle should be built from `editorial proof paragraphs`.

Each proof paragraph should:

- make one claim
- support that claim with two or three connected details
- move naturally into the next pressure point

Actor material still matters, but it should appear as evidence inside the paragraph, not as a profile template.

## Voice Rule

The narrator should sound:

- cold
- clean
- authoritative
- lightly magazine-like
- never chatty
- never academic

The voice should prefer:

- compressed judgement
- sharp transitions
- controlled emphasis

The voice should avoid:

- repeated structural labels
- system phrases
- bureaucratic symmetry
- obvious filler cadence

## Transition Rule

The article should stop sounding like separate modules stitched together.

Transitions should feel earned:

- from visible frame to contradiction
- from contradiction to timing
- from timing to proof
- from proof to consequence
- from consequence to ending

The reader should feel pulled through one argument, not moved from slot to slot.

## Ending Rule

The ending should no longer merely restate unresolved questions.

It should:

- sharpen the main pressure one last time
- identify the most dangerous unresolved question
- leave the article on a harder, more memorable sentence

The reader should finish with:

- one clarified insight
- one sharpened risk
- one reason the next development matters more now than before reading

## What Stays Stable

No change to:

- dossier fields
- doctrine baseline
- evaluation categories
- operator pipeline
- publish/review flow
- public article rendering contract

This slice changes flagship writer phrasing, transitions, and paragraph composition only.

## Success Criteria

This pass is successful when a reader reaction shifts from:

- `this is a better structured analysis`

to:

- `this sounds like a real CoreWire article`

That means the text should still carry the same insight density, but it must stop feeling system-generated in its cadence and paragraph shape.
